Case study · Payments concept · 5-day sprint

EventPlus: Paid events INSIDE Google Calendar.

Role — User research + final design on 4 of 9 screens Team — 5, spanning Product, Tech, Marketing, Business, Design Format — Conceptual feature, not affiliated with Google
Summary
The problem
Scheduling is solved; monetizing isn't. Anyone running a paid event has to leave Calendar and stitch together separate tools for ticketing, payment, and promotion — a workflow Google's own ecosystem sits adjacent to but never captures.
My role
User research, plus final design on four screens: Entry, Pricing & Tickets, Payment, and Premium. The case study shows all nine screens for full-flow context — attribution below marks exactly which are mine.
The solution
A "Paid Event" toggle inside Calendar's existing create flow: tiered ticketing with live net-revenue math, inline UPI/bank setup with visible trust cues, QR tickets on publish, and an optional premium tier — core stays free.
The honest caveat
A five-day concept has no live usage data. Every metric here is a launch target scoped to what the flow could instrument — labeled as such, never presented as a result.
01 — Problem

Six points of friction

No integrated payments. No built-in ticketing. No premium tier for larger events. No monetized promotion through Google's own ecosystem. A fragmented workflow where constant tool-switching causes drop-off. And no sector-specific flows for webinars, workshops, or meetups. Each friction point became a design requirement — from a single "Paid Event" toggle to one unified dashboard for sales, attendees, and tasks.

02 — Grounding

Learning from a competitor's mistakes

Rather than inventing requirements, we anchored decisions to observed usability failures in Cisco Webex's event experience.

Webex issueHeuristic violatedEventPlus response
Cluttered dashboardAesthetic & minimalist designOne dedicated "+ Create" CTA; no competing actions.
Extra pre-join clicksUser control & freedomLinear modal workflow — no pop-outs or screen hops.
Poor visual hierarchyRecognition over recallTabbed modal + summary panel anchors the primary action.
Inconsistent entry pointsConsistency & standardsUnified "+ Create" entry; paid is a clear secondary tab.

04 — The flow, slide by slide

Nine screens. Four are mine.

Each screen below is tagged the way the deck tags it: My work for my individual design, Team build for screens designed by teammates. Mine carry the reasoning; the team's carry context.

01 · Entry

My work

Every event starts from the “+ Create” button people already use — the paid-event toggle reveals its fields inline. New capability, zero new navigation to learn. This was the direct answer to Webex's inconsistent entry points.

02 · Customize

Team build

Sector-specific event type and dynamic fields, built by the team's product and tech lens.

03 · Pricing & Tickets

My work

Organizers price events in spreadsheets because tools show gross numbers. The tier builder shows net revenue live — gross, commission, and total update as you type — so the pricing decision happens where the pricing input is.

04 · Payment

My work

Trust cues placed at the moment of maximum hesitation: PCI badge above the fold, fraud, encryption and storage signals at the point of authorization. UPI-first for the Indian market, bank account as the visible alternative, no external redirects.

05 · Registration

Team build

Auto-generated invite email and QR ticket, previewed before publish.

06 · Published

Team build

Confirmation screen with an optional upsell into paid promotion, kept separate from publishing itself.

07 · Premium

My work

Three tiers with the middle plan visually anchored — and the structural decision that mattered: every core paid-event feature stays free, so the upsell never gates adoption.

08 · Amplify

Team build

One-click campaign setup across Google Ads, YouTube, and Gmail with a budget slider and reach estimate.

09 · Confirmation & Boost

Team build

Back in Calendar — live sales, tasks, and attendee tracking in one panel.

05 — Result & reflection

How success would be measured

A five-day concept has no live usage data — these two metrics are scoped to exactly what this flow can instrument at launch:

Target — not a result

End-to-end flow completion

Baseline ~0% (no in-Calendar paid support today). Target: ≥50% complete Create → Toggle → Pricing → Payment → Publish.

Target — not a result

Registration conversion

Baseline ~10% (external pages, poor attribution). Target: ≥25% via the internal "Amplify" channel.

What I would do next: validate the flow with 5–8 real organizers (payment and pricing first), pressure-test feasibility with engineering, and prototype the mobile version.
